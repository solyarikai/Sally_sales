### What Alpha Got RIGHT That We Missed

1. **AFFPERF campaign launch.** We completely missed this. Alpha identified that impact.com (a strong fit!) is literally an affiliate/performance company that came through the wrong campaign. Hundreds of affiliate platforms (Partnerize, Awin, CJ Affiliate, Rakuten, etc.) are untouched. This is a proven segment with zero dedicated effort -- a genuine blind spot in our analysis.

2. **"No" list.** Alpha recommends defining companies that should NOT be contacted (influData because they have their own data, HypeAuditor as a competitor). We flagged competitive positioning but didn't take the practical step of creating a block list. Simple, high-impact.

3. **Week-over-week trend analysis.** Alpha noted that Week 4 shows declining reply rates (1.11% vs. higher in Weeks 1-2), raising the question of list fatigue or sequence degradation. We didn't track this temporal decline.

4. **Response time hypothesis.** Alpha proposed tracking "first reply timestamp" and hypothesized that <2 hour responses convert at 2-3x the rate. We recommended the 4-hour SLA but didn't frame it as a testable hypothesis with measurement.

5. **WANNA TALK tiering by role.** We recommended ABM for all 19. Alpha split them into Tier 1 (7 decision-makers) and Tier 2 (assistants/EAs to use as access points). This is more operationally practical.

### What Alpha Got WRONG

1. **PR firms reply rate correction is wrong.** Alpha claims the "real" PR firms reply rate is 1.2% (3 replies from 242 sent), not 0.2% (from 1,000 contacts). This is misleading. The 0.2% is the correct denominator because 1,000 contacts were loaded and the campaign is actively sending to them -- the fact that only 242 have been sent so far doesn't change the total investment. More importantly, Alpha uses this to say "Don't kill this segment yet" -- but the recommendation should be the opposite. With only 242 sent and already seeing only 3 replies (zero interested), the early signal is WEAK, not neutral. Alpha is giving PR firms an undeserved reprieve based on statistical optimism.

2. **"Scale" verdict for the flagship campaign is premature.** Alpha says "Double contact volume, same sequence." But the flagship campaign's 6.2% reply rate likely includes older, higher-quality leads that have now been exhausted. Week-over-week decline (which Alpha themselves identified) suggests that simply doubling volume will produce diminishing returns. The recommendation should be "maintain volume, improve conversion" not "double volume."

3. **Cost-per-meeting calculation is incomplete.** Alpha estimates $38-97 per meeting based on email platform costs only. This misses SDR time (Nastya's hours), lead sourcing costs (Apollo/Crona credits), and Sally's agency time. The real cost per meeting is likely 3-5x higher. This matters because it changes the unit economics argument for segment allocation.

4. **Reply scripts lack the "two options" structure.** Alpha's scripts all funnel toward a call. Our scripts and Delta's scripts offer alternatives ("I can send data async" or "send me a creator handle and I'll pull it"). For technical buyers (CTOs), offering a no-call path increases response rates significantly.

### What's MISSING from Alpha

1. **No contrarian takes.** Alpha plays it safe. No challenge to the 450M number as a marketing claim, no questioning of whether LinkedIn is the right channel for this ICP, no suggestion of "reverse demos." Alpha diagnoses and prescribes but doesn't push the team to rethink assumptions.

2. **No discussion of A/B testing validity.** We flagged that the team lacks volume for statistical significance. Alpha mentions tracking A/B results but doesn't address whether the testing protocol is even viable at current volumes.

3. **No LinkedIn script content.** Alpha identifies LinkedIn as broken but provides no actual LinkedIn reply scripts. Just "Add calendar link" -- which is necessary but insufficient.

4. **No competitive positioning matrix.** Alpha mentions competitors but doesn't build a comparison document. Both we and Delta provided one.

5. **Missing analysis of the "interested vs. warm" categorization problem.** We identified 5 warm/misclassified leads; Alpha groups them but doesn't call out the categorization system as broken.
