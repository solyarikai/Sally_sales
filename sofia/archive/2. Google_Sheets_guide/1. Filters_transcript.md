## Content

Now imagine that you have a situation when you have duplicates in your database and you want to look for unique rows. So you just select your table, go to **Data** and **Data cleanup**. After that you click **Remove duplicates**. Right here you choose the columns that need to be analyzed.

## Removing Duplicates

For example, you want to delete rows that are completely duplicated — meaning all columns in those rows are the same. You just click "Select all." Look at this example: you can see that Mira Shah is duplicated three times — they are basically identical. So we just click "Remove duplicates" and all the unnecessary duplicated rows are removed.

## Using Filters

Another important feature is filters. Filters allow you to create the view that you want. For example, you want to target only SaaS companies — so you just leave "SaaS" selected.

Or, for example, you *don't* want to target SaaS companies. You filter your entire base and then delete those leads. It's important **not to delete the cell content** — you should **delete the row**. Just right-click and choose "Delete row." After that, you remove the filter. See, there are no SaaS companies left.

We click Ctrl+Z to undo everything for now.

## Excluding One List from Another Using VLOOKUP

Also, an important use case for the VLOOKUP function is when you want to exclude one list from another. Imagine you have a really big list of companies and you want to make sure there are no intersections with another list.

In this scenario, select these companies, click **Data > Remove duplicates**. Let's say you also have Amazon and Facebook in the list. We want to exclude companies from our main list that are *also* in this second list.

So we use the VLOOKUP function: select the lookup column, select the range (protect it with dollar signs), and choose only the first column. Click `0` as always. You can see that "Acorn Labs" exists in the lookup list. For Google, Amazon, and Facebook — they don't exist in the other list, so you get `#N/A`.

After that, you can easily apply your filter — filter for `#N/A` rows and delete those companies. Remove the filter, and you have your list cleaned. You only have the contacts and companies that exist in the desired list.

That's also a scenario for when you want to exclude one list from another.
