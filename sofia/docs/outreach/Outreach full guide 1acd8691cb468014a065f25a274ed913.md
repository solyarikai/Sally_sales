## 6. Best Practices

We’ve already covered the basics (warm-up, limits, reputation), but here’s a quick list of things you *shouldn’t* do — treat it like your outreach survival kit.

**🔗 LinkedIn:**

- Don’t go over daily connection limits — even on aged accounts, keep it under 50/day. Safe zone is 30–40.
- Avoid blasting the *same exact message* to dozens of people in a row. Add variation. Change intros, angles, or at least the wording.

- Never use shady scripts or browser extensions — stick to trusted tools like Linked Helper. Unverified plugins = high risk of ban.
- Keep an eye on your **SSI (Social Selling Index)** and how people are reacting to your messages. If too many mark you as “Spam,” LinkedIn will throttle or block you fast.
- Target carefully: send to people who are likely to be genuinely interested, and be polite. If LinkedIn flags your account (e.g. asks for ID or shows a warning), pause activity for a few days and slow down after that.

**📧 Email:**

- The most common ban comes from your email provider (e.g. Google Workspace suspending outgoing mail for suspected spam).
- To avoid this:
    
    a) **Always warm up inboxes**
    
    b) **Ramp up gradually** — don’t send hundreds of emails on Day 1
    
    c) **Spread load across multiple inboxes/domains** — it’s way safer to send 50 emails from 5 accounts than 250 from one
    
    d) **Monitor complaints** — if someone asks to be removed, respect that immediately. Use Unsubscribe links or remove them manually.
    
- Goodwill matters. Stay respectful — there’s a real person on the other end, not just an “email target.”

**🌐 Domain reputation:**

- For heavy outreach, consider using a **secondary domain** that looks similar to your main one — for example, if your website is `company.com`, use something like `company.co` or `trycompany.com` for cold emails.
- This helps protect your main domain’s reputation in case something goes wrong.
- Still, this isn’t a silver bullet — you’ll still need to warm it up and send quality content. If your emails are bad, no domain will save you.

Not every reply will be a “yes” — and that’s totally normal. Here’s how to handle common types of rejections and turn them into future opportunities (or at least leave a good impression).

- **Short rejection (“not interested”):**
    
    Keep it polite and brief. Something like:
    
    *“Totally understand — thanks for getting back to me! If you ever need [your service] down the line, happy to reconnect. Have a great day!”*
    
    Don’t push back or try to convince them — it’ll only create friction.
    
- **“We already have a provider/solution”:**
    
    Great — acknowledge it positively:
    
    *“Good to hear you're covered! If you ever need a backup option or if anything changes, happy to stay in touch.”*
    
    You *can* ask (tactfully) if they’re fully satisfied with the current solution — sometimes that opens up a conversation, but read the tone before doing so.
    
- **“Send more info”:**
    
    That’s not a yes, but it’s not a no either. Send what they asked for (a PDF, pitch deck, case studies) — and don’t forget to suggest a follow-up:
    
    *“Here’s a quick overview attached. Would be happy to discuss in more detail — would Friday or early next week work for a quick chat?”*
    
    If they go quiet, follow up a few days later:
    
    *“Just checking if you had a chance to look at the info — would it make sense to revisit the conversation?”*
    
- **Angry reply or clear opt-out (“remove me from your list”):**
    
    Happens. Stay professional and respectful:
    
    *“Sorry for the interruption — won’t reach out again. Wishing you all the best!”*
    
    Don’t argue, don’t explain — just remove them from your list. A single bad interaction can do more damage than 10 good ones.
    
- **No response at all:**
    
    This is the most common outcome. Don’t take it personally 🙂 Usually, you’ll send 1 main email + 2 follow-ups. If no reply after 3 touchpoints, it’s okay to stop — or try another channel like LinkedIn or a call. On the phone, you can say:
    
    *“I sent you a quick email earlier — just wanted to follow up directly in case it didn’t go through.”*
    
    Some leads respond better to voice. You can also “revive” cold leads months later with a new offer, update, or case study. That’s why using a CRM and tagging leads by status (“no reply”, “not interested”, “follow up later”) is super helpful in the long run.
    

Let’s wrap up with what separates high-performing campaigns from those that flop.

- **Solid preparation:** The best results always come from doing the groundwork — clean lists, warmed-up domains, and well-crafted messages. One agency spent two full weeks researching and personalizing before launching a 500-contact campaign. Result? 55% open rate and 15% reply rate — way above average. On the flip side, throwing together a messy list and blasting a generic template? You’ll get a handful of replies — or a ban.
- **Real personalization & value:** In one SaaS campaign, the team wrote 100 custom emails — each with a tailored insight pulled from company reports. Response rate? ~30%, and half of those turned into calls. Was it time-consuming? Definitely. But the conversion rate was close to warm leads. Lesson: fewer contacts + higher relevance = better outcomes.
- **Multi-channel is king:** Don’t rely on just one channel. Email + LinkedIn combo works great. Example: send an email, then follow up on LinkedIn with a message like *“Just sent you a quick note — wanted to make sure it didn’t get lost.”* Then another email follow-up. This “omni-channel” flow increases chances of engagement — just be careful not to overwhelm them on all platforms at once.
- **Data-driven learning:** Top performers constantly look at their metrics and adjust. If something’s not working — change the offer, rethink the audience, or test a new angle. Follow what’s trending — right now, informal tone and **video intros** are gaining traction (some reps include short personal videos in the first email). Learn from what others are doing — case studies, playbooks, community chats — and iterate fast.

### ⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Sending without checking your list** — low quality = low results (or worse, blacklisting).
- **Wall-of-text emails** — nobody’s reading long cold intros. Keep it short and sharp.
- **Tricking the reader** — fake “Re:” threads, misleading subject lines like “Your invoice”… Sure, they get opens, but they also kill trust. Be creative, not clickbaity.
    - **Skipping follow-ups** — most replies come on the 2nd or 3rd touch. Don’t stop after one try.
- **Not learning** — what worked 2 years ago may not work today. Keep growing: read, test, share insights with your team, join sales communities (like Reddit’s /r/sales or Telegram groups). The best reps are always improving.
